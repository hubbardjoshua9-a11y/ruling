## Unit Overview

Almost every job today involves a computer and business software. Booking appointments, updating a spreadsheet, writing a letter, recording a sale, saving a file so a colleague can find it next week: all of this is using business software applications.

This unit teaches you how to do that everyday computer-based work well: safely, accurately, and the way your organisation expects. You do not need to be a computer expert. The unit is pitched at someone doing routine office and administrative tasks, usually under supervision, in a real or simulated workplace.

By the end of this unit you will be able to:

- get set up safely at your workstation and understand a task before you start
- choose the right software for a job and open it correctly
- enter, check, format and fix information in an application
- finalise, name and store your work so it can be found and trusted later.

The guide has four modules. Module 1 sets you up to work safely and clearly. Modules 2 to 4 then follow the natural flow of any computer task from start to finish: choosing your tool, doing the work, and closing out cleanly.

## Module 1: Set up for safe, productive work

Before you touch a keyboard, two things make the rest of your work easier: understanding what you have actually been asked to do, and setting yourself up so you can work comfortably and safely. This module covers both.

### 1.1 Understanding the task before you start

Jumping straight into a document and working out what it should be as you go usually means redoing it. A few minutes spent understanding the task first saves that. When you are given a task, read the instruction or brief and identify four things:

- **Purpose**: what the task is for. Is it to inform a customer, record a sale, prepare a report, or ask someone for a decision?
- **Audience**: who will read or use the finished work. A manager, a customer, a supplier, or the whole team? The audience changes how formal it needs to be.
- **Format**: what the finished work should be. A printed letter, an email, a spreadsheet, a slide deck, or a form?
- **Presentation requirements**: any rules about how it should look. Many organisations have a template, a logo, a standard font, or a required date format.

If anything is unclear, ask before you start, not after. A good clarifying question is specific. "Should the price update go out as a printed letter or as an email to our trade customers?" gets you a useful answer. "What do I do with this?" does not. Checking with the right person, usually your supervisor, is a normal part of the job, not a sign you cannot cope.

**Examples across industries**

- In a **real estate agency**, an administrator is asked to "let the tenants know about the inspection". The purpose is to inform, the audience is tenants, and the format is most likely an email or letter using the agency's template.
- In a **community health centre**, a receptionist is asked to "put together the new patient pack". The purpose is to give new patients information, the audience is patients, and the format is a printed booklet using the centre's branding.
- On a **construction project**, a site administrator is asked to "get the sign-in sheet ready for Monday". The purpose is to record who is on site, the audience is site workers and the supervisor, and the format is a printed sheet on the company template.

> **Activity 1.1 (practice only, not assessed)**
> You are asked to "send the new price list to our trade customers." Write down the purpose, the audience, and the likely format. Then write one specific question you would ask your supervisor to clarify the task. Activities like this are for your own practice; they are not marked. Ask your trainer if you would like feedback.

### 1.2 Your workstation and your body

Working at a computer for long periods can cause discomfort or injury (sore eyes, a stiff neck, aching wrists) if your workspace is not set up well. Setting up your workstation to suit your body is called *ergonomics*. It only takes a moment and it makes a real difference, especially if you share a desk with others.

Aim for a relaxed, upright posture with your shoulders loose and your upper arms close to your body. Adjust:

- **Chair**: feet flat on the floor (or on a footrest), thighs roughly horizontal, lower back supported by the chair.
- **Monitor**: directly in front of you, about an arm's length away, with the top of the screen at or just above eye level so you are not tilting your head up or down. Tilt the screen back slightly and angle it away from windows and lights so you are not fighting glare.
- **Keyboard and mouse**: directly in front of you and close, so your elbows stay near your sides and your wrists stay flat and straight rather than bent.
- **Desk**: a fixed sitting desk is usually around 680 to 720 mm high. If it is height-adjustable, set it so your forearms are roughly level when you type.

Then build in small breaks. Look away from the screen and change position regularly, roughly every 30 to 60 minutes. These small adjustments prevent most everyday computer-related aches.

**Examples across industries**

- A **local council** customer service team uses hot-desks. Each morning an officer re-raises the chair, drops the monitor to eye level and pulls the keyboard close, because the person who used the desk yesterday was much taller.
- In an **accounting firm** during tax season, staff who are at the screen all day set a quiet reminder to stand and stretch every hour to avoid neck and wrist strain.
- At a **manufacturing company**, the office administrator angles her monitor away from a large window so afternoon sun does not create glare she has to squint through.

> **Activity 1.2 (practice only, not assessed)**
> Use the four points above to check your own workstation right now. Note one adjustment you made. This is practice only and is not marked.

### 1.3 Work health and safety for an office role

Under Australia's work health and safety laws (the model Work Health and Safety Act 2011, adopted in most states and territories), every worker has a duty to take reasonable care of their own health and safety, to take reasonable care that what they do does not harm others, and to follow reasonable safety instructions at work. For a computer-based role, meeting that duty mostly comes down to a few everyday habits:

- set up your workstation properly, as in the previous section
- take regular screen breaks
- keep your work area and cables tidy, and keep leads out of walkways
- report any hazard or near miss (a frayed power lead, a wobbly chair, a trip hazard) to your supervisor straight away.

Work health and safety is overseen nationally by Safe Work Australia, and enforced by the regulator in your state or territory (for example WorkSafe Victoria, SafeWork NSW, WorkSafe Queensland, or Comcare for many Australian Government workplaces). Your own organisation will have a work health and safety policy and a way to report hazards, usually a form or an online report. Knowing where both are, before you need them, is part of doing the job well.

**Examples across industries**

- A new administration assistant at a **community health centre** notices a frayed power lead under the reception desk. Rather than keep using it, she reports it on the centre's hazard report form so it can be replaced.
- In a **retail head office**, a staff member sees boxes stacked across a walkway near the printers and moves them, then mentions it to the office manager so it does not happen again.
- At a **logistics company's** office, an administrator who starts getting headaches raises it with her supervisor, who arranges an anti-glare adjustment and a short screen-break routine.

> **Activity 1.3 (practice only, not assessed)**
> Name two work health and safety hazards that could exist at an office workstation, and describe how you would report each one in your workplace. Not marked, for your own practice.

## Module 2: Choosing the right software for the job

There are dozens of programs on a typical work computer, but most everyday tasks only need a handful. The skill is knowing what each common application is *for*, so you can pick the right one quickly and open it the way your organisation expects.

### 2.1 The common business applications and what each is for

Most workplaces use a small set of application types. You do not need to master all of them; you need to know which one suits which job:

- **Word processor** (for example Microsoft Word or Google Docs): letters, reports, memos, and any document that will be read or printed as text.
- **Spreadsheet** (for example Microsoft Excel or Google Sheets): lists, numbers, calculations, budgets, and anything you need to sort or total.
- **Email and calendar** (for example Microsoft Outlook or Gmail): sending messages, attaching files, and scheduling meetings.
- **Presentation** (for example Microsoft PowerPoint or Google Slides): slides for a talk, a meeting, or an update shown on screen.
- **Database or records system** (for example a customer management system, a patient records system, or a student records system): storing structured records so they can be searched and updated.
- **PDF reader and creator** (for example Adobe Acrobat): fixed-format documents for sharing, printing or signing, where you do not want the layout to change.
- **Cloud file storage** (for example SharePoint, OneDrive or Google Drive): storing and sharing files online so colleagues can reach them from anywhere.

These are the applications used for organising electronic information and data in most Australian workplaces. Some organisations use the Microsoft set, some use Google's, and many use a mix; the *types* are the same even when the brand differs.

**Working with AI in business software**

Most of these applications now include an AI assistant — for example Microsoft Copilot in Word and Excel, or Gemini in Google Workspace. In current workplaces, producing a routine document increasingly means *directing* an AI tool to draft it and then *checking and correcting* the result, rather than typing every word from scratch. The skill that matters is your judgment over the output: does the draft match the brief, the house style and the facts? You stay accountable for what you send, so you read the AI's draft critically, fix what is wrong, and only then submit it. This is why Assessment 2 asks you to draft the welcome memo with an AI tool and then evaluate and correct what it produces — the competency being assessed is governing the tool, not the AI's ability to write a memo.

### 2.2 Matching the application to the task

The quickest way to choose is to ask: *what does the finished work need to do?*

- Needs to calculate, sort or total numbers → **spreadsheet**.
- Needs to be read or printed as a formatted document → **word processor**.
- Needs to be shown on screen to a group → **presentation software**.
- Needs to store records you will look up and update later → **database or records system**.
- Needs to be sent to someone, with a file attached → **email**.

If two tools could both do the job, choose the one your organisation normally uses for that task. Choosing well the first time avoids rework. For example, starting a list of stock in a word processor and then having to rebuild it in a spreadsheet when you realise it needs to add up.

**Examples across industries**

- A **real estate agency** keeps its rent roll in a spreadsheet because the rents need to total and sort, but writes a tenant notice in a word processor because it needs to read and print as a letter.
- A **primary school** office looks up a parent's phone number in the student records system rather than searching old emails, because the records system is the reliable source.
- A **not-for-profit** prepares a quarterly update for its board in presentation software, because it will be shown on a screen in the meeting.

### 2.3 Opening the right application the organisation's way

Organisations set up their software differently. Some have applications installed on the computer; others run them in a web browser as cloud apps. To open an application:

- find it where your organisation keeps it: the desktop, the Start menu, the taskbar, or a saved bookmark in the browser
- sign in with your organisational account if you are asked to
- then open the correct existing file, or start a new one, as the task requires.

If you are not sure which system your organisation uses for a task (for example, whether leave is recorded in email or in an HR system), ask. Using the right system from the start keeps everyone's records in one place.

**Examples across industries**

- A **hospitality venue** administrator opens the bookings system from a browser bookmark and signs in with her venue account, rather than keeping bookings in a personal spreadsheet.
- A **construction company** office uses installed Microsoft programs on the office computers, so the site administrator opens Word from the taskbar to start a site notice.
- A **government department** officer opens the records management system from the desktop to file correspondence, because email is not where official records are kept.

## Module 3: Doing the work: enter, check, format and fix

This module is the core of the unit: actually working inside an application. The cycle is the same whatever the program: put the information in, check it, make it look right, and deal with any small problems that come up.

### 3.1 Entering information and data accurately

Once the application is open, enter the information the task and your organisation require. Accuracy matters more than speed. A fast but wrong entry has to be found and fixed later, often by someone else.

- Work from the source. Whatever you were given (a brief, a form, an email, a stack of figures), keep it in front of you and enter from it.
- Follow your organisation's conventions. Many workplaces have rules about date formats, capitalisation, which fields are required, and how names or codes are written.
- Enter one section at a time and glance back at the source to confirm before moving on, rather than racing to the end and hoping.

**Examples across industries**

- A **medical practice** administrator entering new patient details checks each field against the registration form, because an error in a date of birth or Medicare number causes real problems later.
- A **warehouse** office worker entering a stock delivery types the quantities straight from the delivery docket, ticking each line as she goes.
- A **community services** organisation's intake officer follows the required format for client names and reference numbers so records match across the system.

### 3.2 Checking and amending your work

Never assume the first version is right. Build in a check before you treat anything as finished:

- Read it back against the source you entered from.
- Use the application's own tools. Word processors have spellcheck and grammar check. Spreadsheets can flag errors and can use data validation to stop wrong entries. Use them, but do not rely on them blindly, because a spellchecker will happily accept "their" where you meant "there".
- Fix what is wrong, then re-check the part you changed, because corrections sometimes introduce new mistakes.

**Examples across industries**

- A **retail head office** assistant proofreads a price list against the supplier's email before it goes out, catching a $19 that should have been $90.
- A **school** office worker uses spellcheck on a newsletter, then still reads it once more because spellcheck did not notice "from" where "form" was meant.
- An **accounting firm** junior turns on data validation in a spreadsheet so a date column will not accept text by mistake.

### 3.3 Formatting so it is fit for purpose

Formatting makes information clear, consistent and professional. Use each application's formatting features appropriately for the task, and use your organisation's template or house style where one exists, so your work matches everything else that goes out.

- In a **word processor**: use headings, bold, bullet or numbered lists, and alignment to make a document easy to read. Apply the organisation's template rather than inventing your own look.
- In a **spreadsheet**: set sensible column widths, format numbers as currency or dates where needed, add borders or shading to separate sections, and use simple totals.
- In a **presentation**: keep a consistent slide layout and a font size big enough to read from across a room.

Good formatting is not decoration. A well-formatted run-sheet or price list is faster and safer for the next person to use.

**Examples across industries**

- A **hotel or function venue** administrator formats an event run-sheet with clear headings and times so floor staff can read it at a glance during service.
- A **government** office applies the department's document template to a report so it looks consistent with everything else the department publishes.
- A **trade supplies** business formats a quote in its standard template, with the logo, totals and GST line in the expected places.

### 3.4 When something goes wrong: help functions and simple faults

Small problems are a normal part of working with software. Two skills handle most of them.

**Use the built-in help.** Almost every application has a Help menu, a search box (often labelled "Tell me what you want to do" or shown as a magnifying glass), and tooltips that pop up to explain what a button does. Searching the exact thing you are stuck on (for example "how to merge cells" or "how to add page numbers") usually finds a clear answer faster than asking around.

**Identify and address simple faults.** Common faults include an application that freezes, a file that will not open, a printer that will not respond, or a spreadsheet cell showing an error such as `#####` or `#REF!`. Try the simple fixes first:

- save your work (if you can) so nothing is lost
- close the application and reopen it
- check you opened the right file in the right place
- read the on-screen error message, which often says what is wrong (for example `#####` in a spreadsheet usually just means the column is too narrow to show the number, so widening the column fixes it).

**Stepping up (Certificate III).** Handling a fault well also means knowing **when to fix it yourself and when to escalate**. If the simple fixes do not work, or the problem could affect other people or risk losing data, do not keep pushing. Log what happened (what you were doing, what the error said, and what you already tried) and report it to your supervisor or IT support. A clear note helps whoever fixes it solve the problem faster, and stops a small issue becoming a lost afternoon.

**Examples across industries**

- A **logistics** office worker sees `#####` across a stock-count spreadsheet, realises the columns are too narrow, widens them, and the numbers reappear, with no need to escalate.
- A **medical practice** administrator finds the patient records system will not load. She notes the error message, tries closing and reopening once, then logs an IT ticket rather than guessing, because patient data is involved.
- A **construction** site administrator cannot get the site printer to respond before a 7am start. He checks it is on and connected, then calls IT support and uses a different printer in the meantime so the sign-in sheet is ready.

> **Activity 3 (practice only, not assessed)**
> A spreadsheet cell shows `#####` instead of a number. Describe two things you would try to fix it, and describe one situation where you would stop and escalate to your supervisor or IT support instead. Not marked, for your own practice.

## Module 4: Finalise, name and store your work

The last few minutes of a task matter as much as the first. A file that is well checked, sensibly named and stored in the right place can be found and trusted weeks later. One that is saved to the desktop with a vague name is effectively lost. This module covers closing out cleanly.

### 4.1 The final review and edit pass

Before you save and share, do one last review of the whole thing:

- read it through once more for any errors or anything you missed
- confirm it still meets the purpose, audience and format you identified back at the start
- make your final edits, and remove anything that should not be there, draft notes to yourself, test data, or a comment that was only meant for you.

This final pass is quick, but it is the difference between sending out finished work and sending out a draft by mistake.

**Examples across industries**

- A **retail head office** assistant rereads a staff notice and removes the line "check these dates with Sam??" before it goes to all stores.
- A **government** officer deletes the sample figures she used to test a spreadsheet before saving the real version.
- A **hospitality** administrator checks the function run-sheet one last time against the booking before printing it for the team.

### 4.2 File naming conventions and why they matter

A good file name lets anyone find the right file later without opening it. The single most useful habit is to be **consistent**. Where you include a date, write it as **YYYY-MM-DD**: for example 2026-06-04. This is the only date order that automatically sorts files into correct chronological order in a folder, so the newest version sits where you expect it.

Beyond the date, keep the rest of the name consistent too:

- include a short, clear description of the content (for example `Trade-Customer-Price-List`)
- show the version where it matters (`v1`, `v2`, or `FINAL`) so the latest one is never in doubt
- avoid characters some systems reject, such as `/ \ : * ? " < >`.

A practical pattern is date, then description, then version:
`2026-06-04_Trade-Customer-Price-List_v2.xlsx`

Always follow your organisation's naming standard where it has one. Many workplaces, especially in government and records-heavy industries, have a set convention you are expected to use.

**Examples across industries**

- A **government department** applies a strict records file-naming standard so any file can be traced and retrieved years later.
- An **accounting firm** names every client working file with the YYYY-MM-DD date and a version number, so the most recent file is never in question at tax time.
- A **retail** head office keeps a tidy shared-drive folder structure with consistent file names, so staff across different stores can find the current price list.

> Rewrite this file name correctly using a date-led, versioned convention: "june newsletter final final (2)". Not marked, for your own practice.

### 4.3 Storing, backing up and exiting safely

Once the file is named, store it properly and close down cleanly:

- **Save to the correct location**: the right folder on the shared drive or in cloud storage, not just the desktop where only you can find it.
- **Use the expected folder structure**: put the file where a colleague would think to look for it.
- **Know your backup**: organisational cloud storage usually backs up automatically, but check whether yours does, especially for anything important.
- **Mind privacy**: if a file contains people's personal information (names, contact details, customer or staff records), handle it carefully. Australian privacy law (the Privacy Act 1988 and the Australian Privacy Principles) expects organisations to keep personal information secure and to use it only for the purpose it was collected for. In practice, save such files to the secure location your organisation specifies, do not email them or copy them somewhere unprotected, and follow your organisation's privacy or data-handling policy.
- **Exit properly**: save, then close the file and exit the application, rather than just shutting the laptop lid and hoping it is saved.

**Examples across industries**

- A **medical practice** administrator saves patient contact updates straight into the secure practice records system, rather than keeping a copy on the desktop.
- A **community services** worker stores client case notes in the organisation's secure case system and closes it when she leaves her desk, because the information is sensitive.
- A **logistics** office worker saves the day's stock spreadsheet to the shared operations folder so the morning shift can open it, then exits the program properly at the end of the day.

> **Activity 4.3 (practice only, not assessed)**
> Name one file you might create that contains personal information, and describe where it should be stored and one thing you would do to keep it secure. Not marked, for your own practice.

### Assessment Readiness: Module 4

Before moving on, you should be able to:

- carry out a final review and edit before saving, and remove anything that should not be in the file
- name a file using a clear, consistent, date-led and versioned convention
- store a file in the correct location with the expected folder structure, handle personal information securely, and exit the application properly.

## How this unit builds your foundation skills

This unit develops the everyday skills that sit underneath the software tasks:

- **Reading**: you interpret task briefs and instructions from familiar sources to work out what a job needs (Modules 1 and 2).
- **Writing**: you produce and amend files, and complete documents using your organisation's formats and templates (Modules 3 and 4).
- **Digital literacy**: the whole unit builds your confidence in selecting, using, troubleshooting and managing common business software.
- **Self-management**: you set yourself up to work safely, check your own work, and know when to ask for help or escalate a problem.

You do not need to study these separately. They develop naturally as you work through the modules and the activities.

## Wrapping up

Using business software well is not about knowing every feature of every program. It is about working in an orderly way: understand the task, set yourself up safely, choose the right tool, enter and check your work carefully, make it look right, and finish by naming and storing the file so it can be found and trusted. Do that consistently and you will be a reliable, valued member of any workplace team.

When you are ready, talk to your assessor about the assessment for this unit. It is in a separate workbook and gives you the chance to show these skills in a simulated workplace (or your own workplace, where your assessor agrees that is suitable).
