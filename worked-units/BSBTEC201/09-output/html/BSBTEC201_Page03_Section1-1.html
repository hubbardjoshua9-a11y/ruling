1.1 – Understanding the task before you start

Jumping straight into a document and working out what it should be as you go usually means redoing it. A few minutes spent understanding the task first saves that. When you are given a task, read the instruction or brief and identify four things:

  • Purpose: what the task is for. Is it to inform a customer, record a sale, prepare a report, or ask someone for a decision?
  • Audience: who will read or use the finished work. A manager, a customer, a supplier, or the whole team? The audience changes how formal it needs to be.
  • Format: what the finished work should be. A printed letter, an email, a spreadsheet, a slide deck, or a form?
  • Presentation requirements: any rules about how it should look. Many organisations have a template, a logo, a standard font, or a required date format.

If anything is unclear, ask before you start, not after. A good clarifying question is specific. "Should the price update go out as a printed letter or as an email to our trade customers?" gets you a useful answer. "What do I do with this?" does not. Checking with the right person, usually your supervisor, is a normal part of the job, not a sign you cannot cope.

Examples across Australian workplaces
    • In a real estate agency, an administrator is asked to "let the tenants know about the inspection". The purpose is to inform, the audience is tenants, and the format is most likely an email or letter using the agency's template.
    • In a community health centre, a receptionist is asked to "put together the new patient pack". The purpose is to give new patients information, the audience is patients, and the format is a printed booklet using the centre's branding.
    • On a construction project, a site administrator is asked to "get the sign-in sheet ready for Monday". The purpose is to record who is on site, the audience is site workers and the supervisor, and the format is a printed sheet on the company template.

    Activity 1.1 (practice only, not assessed)

    This activity is optional and not assessed. You can ask your assessor to review your answers, but it is not marked.

    You are asked to "send the new price list to our trade customers." Write down the purpose, the audience, and the likely format. Then write one specific question you would ask your supervisor to clarify the task. Activities like this are for your own practice; they are not marked. Ask your trainer if you would like feedback.
