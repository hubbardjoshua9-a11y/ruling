1.3 – Work health and safety for an office role

Under Australia's work health and safety laws (the model Work Health and Safety Act 2011, adopted in most states and territories), every worker has a duty to take reasonable care of their own health and safety, to take reasonable care that what they do does not harm others, and to follow reasonable safety instructions at work. For a computer-based role, meeting that duty mostly comes down to a few everyday habits:

  • set up your workstation properly, as in the previous section
  • take regular screen breaks
  • keep your work area and cables tidy, and keep leads out of walkways
  • report any hazard or near miss (a frayed power lead, a wobbly chair, a trip hazard) to your supervisor straight away.

Work health and safety is overseen nationally by Safe Work Australia, and enforced by the regulator in your state or territory (for example WorkSafe Victoria, SafeWork NSW, WorkSafe Queensland, or Comcare for many Australian Government workplaces). Your own organisation will have a work health and safety policy and a way to report hazards, usually a form or an online report. Knowing where both are, before you need them, is part of doing the job well.

Examples across Australian workplaces
    • A new administration assistant at a community health centre notices a frayed power lead under the reception desk. Rather than keep using it, she reports it on the centre's hazard report form so it can be replaced.
    • In a retail head office, a staff member sees boxes stacked across a walkway near the printers and moves them, then mentions it to the office manager so it does not happen again.
    • At a logistics company's office, an administrator who starts getting headaches raises it with her supervisor, who arranges an anti-glare adjustment and a short screen-break routine.
